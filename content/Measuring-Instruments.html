Instruments are tools of a researcher- each tool is used for a specific purpose.
Tools researchers use to collect data for research studies (alternatively called "tests")
Tools of the data collection which allow the researcher to implement the research method and to produce data for analysis.

Measuring instrument/tools

  • A device used to quantify and record information that is important to a research study.
  • In physical sciences, measuring instruments: scales, rulers, meters etc.
  • In technical education research, these are: tests, questionnaries, observations, interview schedules etc.
          Each of these used to measure (or quantify) and to record the measurement.

Scales of Measurement

Nominal measurement scales

  • Only the assign numbers for purposes of identification and classification; e.g., A1 to teachers and A2 to administrators.
  • Questionaries, document review forms and interview schedules.

Ordinal measurement scales

  • Quantitative qualities that can be used to classify further, i.e., by rank ordering things or people.
  • Only tells rank order and not distance between ranks.
  • Questionaries, opinionaries, observation forms and some teacher-made tests.

Interval Measurement scales

  • Identification and rank order characteristics.
  • Equal intervals between the numbers on the scale.
  • Tests and rating scales.

Ratio measurement scales

  • An interval scale that has an absolute.
  • Only in physical measurement.

Some important terms that are helpful in the further classification.

  • Cognitive tests: Examining subjects' thoughts and thought processes.
  • Affective tests: Examining subjects' feelinggs, interests, attitudes, beliefs, etc.
  • Standardized tests: Tests that are administered, scored and interpreted in a consistent manner.

Measurement instruments

Cognitive instruments

  • Measure an individual's attainment in academic areas typically used to diagnose strengths and weaknesses.
  • Cognitive instruments are of different types: Achievement tests and aptitude tests.
    • Achievement tests
      • Provide information about how well the test takers have learned what they have been taught in institute.
      • Achievement is determined by comparing it to the norm, the performance of group of similar students who have taken the same test.
    • Aptitude tests
      • Measure the intellect ad abilities not normally taught and often are used to predict future performance.

Affective instruments

  • Measure characteristics of individuals along a number of dimensions and to assess fellings, values, and attitudes toward self, others, and variety of other activities, institutions, and situations.
  • Types of affective instruments are: Attitude scales, values tests, personality inventories
    • Attitude scales
      • Self-reports of an individual's beliefs, perceptions, or feelings about self, others, and a variety of activities, institutions, and situations.
      • Frequently use liker, semantic differential, thurstone, or guttman scales.
    • Values tests
      • Measure the relative strength of an individual's valuese of theoretical, economic, asthetic, social, political, and religious values.
    • Personality inventories
      • An individual's self-report measuring how behaviors characteristics of defined personality traits describe that individual.

Projective instruments

Measure a respondent's feelings or thoughts to an ambiguous stimulus.

Standardized instruments

  • They are carefully developed, highly refined measurement procedures, and designed for administration under carefully prescribed conditions for some specific purpose.
  • Include estimates of reliability and validity that are based on sound experiment research.
  • Normative data.
  • E.g., standardized achievement test, aptitude test, ability test, interest inventory, projective measures etc.

Researcher or teacher constructed instruments

  • They are developed for specific research needs where standardized instruments are not available.
  • Need for consistent administration procedures.
  • Consistent scoring procedures.
  • Constructed with reliability and validity in mind.
  • May not be possible to administer the instrument to a large number of people and establish standardized norms.
  • It includes questionnaries, interview schedule, observation form, opinionnarie/attitude scale, document analysis form, teacher-made achievement test.
  • Ensure reliability, validity, consistent administration and scoring procedures.

Tests

Standardized Achievement test

  • Measure what an individual has learned.
  • Developed using sophisticated procedures & undergone extensive tryout & evaluation.
  • Published commercially

Aptitude test

  • Measure an individual's potential or probability of success in a particular activity or occupation, e.g., clerical, mechanical, maths, etc.
  • Emphasis is on future prediction.

Ability test

  • Determine an individual's level of mental functioning or intelligence.
  • Paper & pencil or practical in form.

Interest inventory

  • Assess an individual's interest in something, e.g. career or occupational interests, etc.

Projective Measures

  • Project internal feelings, attitudes, needs values or wishes to an external object.
  • A drawing or a picture and to describe what he sees.

Questionnarie

  • A written instrument containing a series of questions to which individuals respond.
  • Completed independently by an individual.
  • Through mail or personally distributed.
  • Gather factual information.

Strengths

  • Requries a little time of the respondent.
  • Allow for a broad geographic sampling.
  • Low cost of distribution & return.
  • Low research time in administration.

Weaknesses

  • Opportunity to build rapport(a close and harmonious relationship in which the people or groups concerned understand each other's feelings or ideas and communicate well) with the respondents is limited.
  • Ability to probe for details or explanations of responses not possible.
  • Response rrate may be limited.

Designing a questionnarie

  • Intended outcome/purpose of the study, research questions or hypotheses and criterion questions.
  • Brevity; choice of wording; familiar word, no abbreviation; never use double negatives.
  • Underline a word for special emphasis.
  • Phrasing appropriately for all respondents.
  • Types of items:
    • Closed/open
    • Rating
    • Ranking
  • Directions of questionnarie
  • Ordering of items

Interview Schedule

  • Guide the asking of questions.
  • A means for recordng responses made to questions.
  • Factual information/preferences/opinions.
  • Response rate greater
  • Allows an interaction that can facilitate clarification of questions.
  • Interviewer can probe for specific meanings of responses made.

Types

  • Structured: asks respondent an established set of questions.
  • Semi-structured: A general framework for qustioning but not exact set of questions; probing possible.
  • Unstructured: most of speaking by respondents; appropriate for counselling problem solving.

Desinging an interview schedule

  • Determine the content of an interview
  • Developing items for interview schedule (dichotomous response, multiple choice items, rating items, open-ended items).
  • Organizing and assembling interview schedule:
    • Prepare an interview introduction.
    • Order of the interview schedule: first question, last question.
    • Use of funnel sequences (asking the most general or unrestricted question in an area first, and following it with successiveely more restricted questions).
    • Final preparation of interview schedule.

Checklist

Checklists are a list of questions or criteria, which represent the impact areas of an impact assessment or which divide them into their main fields of application. By providing a checklist it is ensured that all relevant impact areas are taken account of by the IA analyst.

Observation Schedules/forms

  • Focus the researcher on the occurance of certain behaviours or objects.
  • Checklist or rating form.

Types

Systematic direct observation

  • Observing people, objects or events and the recording of observations through the use of a highly structured instrument.
  • Researcher must train himself in the process of observation and be familiar with the observation instrument being used.

Participant Observation

  • Such observations by an individual who places himself within a situation, not primarily as a researcher.

Designing an observation form

  • Determine the content (outcome/ purpose of the study, research questions or hypotheses and criterion qustions)
  • Items of an observation form
    • Checklists (list of behaviours or activities that are checked by an O as being observed during a particular observation session).
    • Rating scales
    • Frequency scales
    • Field note forms (sometimes it is needed to record results).
  • Orgainizing and assembling observation form
    • Order of items
    • Final preparation (instructions as guidelines to the O).

Evaluating Research instruments

Selecting an appropriate type of instrument

  • Match between the instrument and research method/questions.
    Descriptive research; questionnaire, interview or document analysis form.
  • What is the relationship between student interest and achievements ?
    Correlational research; an interest inventory and achievement test.

Administering the instrument

  • Make arrangements in advance.
  • Ensure ideal testing environment.
  • Be prepared for all probable contingencies.

Two issues in using instruments

  • Validity: The degree to which the instrument measures what it purports to measure.
  • Reliability: The degree to which the instrument consistency measures what it purposes to measure.

Types of validity

  1. Content validity (the degree to which an instrument measures an intended content area).
    Forms of content validity:
    Sampling validity: does the instrument reflect the total content area ?
    Item validity: Are the items included on the instrument relevant to the measurement of the intended content area ?
  2. Criterion-related validity an individual takes two forms of an instrument which are then correlated to discriminate between those individuals who possess a certain characteristic from those who do not forms of Criterion-related validity.
    Concurrent validity: the degree to which scores on one test correlate to scores on another test when both tests are administered in the same time frame.
    Predictive validity: the degree to which a test can predict how well individual will do in a future situation.
  3. Construct validity a series of studiese validate that the instrument really measures what it purports to measure.

Types of Reliability

  • Stability ("test-retest")>: the degree to which two scores on the same instrument are consistent over time.
  • Equivalence ("equivalent forms"): the degree to which identical instruments (except for the actual items included) yield identical scores.
  • Internal consistency ("split half" reliability with Spearman-Brown correction formula, Kuder-Richardson and Cronback's Alpha reliabilities, scorer/rater reliability) : the degree to which one instrument yields consistent results.
